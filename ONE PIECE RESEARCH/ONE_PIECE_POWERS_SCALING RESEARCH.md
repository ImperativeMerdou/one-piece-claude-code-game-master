# The Power Grammar of One Piece

## Method, canon anchors, and what power means here

Power in One Piece is not a ladder; it’s a language. Scenes feel “right” when multiple dialects talk to each other at once: Devil Fruit body-logic, Haki as social/spiritual pressure, learned technique (martial arts, weapons), science/tech and tools, environment (terrain, weather, water), and public reputation (fear, rumors, headlines). The series repeatedly turns deeds into legends via in‑world storytelling—meaning “who saw it” and “how the world retells it” often matters almost as much as who landed the last hit.

Two canon anchors keep this language from collapsing into generic power creep. First, the official portal’s Devil Fruit rules are deliberately blunt: one bite grants a unique power; you cannot gain a second ability; two identical fruits do not exist at the same time; and “the sea” rejects users—making them unable to swim and weakened even by partial submersion (the page explicitly mentions not just seas but also baths). These constraints are not flavor text; they are structural tension valves.

Second, the fiction treats superpowers as *worldly objects with economics and cruelty attached*. The official portal notes Devil Fruits can sell for at least 100 million berries and are feared by ordinary people, and it even calls out rulers feeding them to enslaved people “for entertainment.” That combination—miracle + commodity + abuse—keeps power morally charged before any fight choreography begins.

Haki is the counterweight: it is framed as spiritual energy/willpower made usable. It is theoretically accessible to all living beings but practically rare without awareness and training; it becomes increasingly common in the New World. It divides into three “colors”: sensing/anticipation (Observation), invisible armor/offense (Armament), and overpowering others’ will (the rare “kingly” type).

Progression is paced through geography. The official story index organizes the series into bounded island arcs and sagas with clear chapter/volume ranges; each “island problem” also functions as a *power problem* (terrain, weather, politics, time limits, specific counters). The same index shows the post‑war two‑year training era as an explicit structural break that resets the baseline without erasing earlier styles.

## The series signature feel: why power stays playful and dangerous

In an SBS Q&A, Eiichiro Oda explicitly links the hero’s power concept to tone control: he says he deliberately picked “the most ridiculous ability” so that even when the story becomes serious, the hero’s very body lets the author “fool around.” This is a core anti‑sludge principle: *comedy is not garnish; it is a stabilizer built into the engine*.

That stabilizer is not “safe.” It is surrounded by danger and disgust. Devil Fruits taste bad, are rare/feared, and—most importantly—cost users their relationship with the sea (in a pirate world, that is existential).

The science-era worldview sharpens this into philosophy: Dr. Vegapunk proposes that each Devil Fruit is the “possibility” of human evolution that someone desired—an embodied “if only I could become like this.” The sea’s rejection becomes moral physics: unnatural wishes can exist, but they carry a punishment.

So the “One Piece power vibe” is a three-way tension that your saga should preserve:

- **Play**: ridiculous bodies, punny moves, humiliation as combat texture.
- **Consequences**: sea weakness, exploitation, discrimination, collateral damage, public fear.
- **Public myth**: what gets named, witnessed, reported, and remembered becomes power.

## The fifteen laws of One Piece power grammar

1. **Power identity law — The move is the person, not a loadout.**
   In this world, “strength” is character writing made kinetic: power expresses what you value, what you refuse, what you fear, and what you protect. The cleanest example is Sanji: he fights almost exclusively with kicks specifically to protect his hands for cooking. The combat rule (“no hands”) is also a moral/professional rule (“I will not sacrifice my craft for violence”), so every fight automatically feels personal and *specific to him*.

   **Design consequence:** give each major fighter one non‑negotiable constraint (ethic, job rule, body rule) that *creates* both limitation and signature solutions. Don’t bolt a personality onto a kit; make the kit *perform* the personality.

2. **Devil Fruit design law — One strong verb, then brutalize it with physics and jokes.**
   The official portal explains Devil Fruits in high-level classes (Paramecia/Zoan/Logia) while treating each ability as singular and identifiable; the pitch is usually one sentence: “become X,” “produce X,” “control X,” “transform into X.”

   What makes them feel native is that simplicity collides immediately with body logic, environment, and improv. The series even bakes in “cartoon pragmatism” like powers extending through clothing: an SBS note explains this was chosen to avoid “unnecessary nudity” if transformations were depicted “realistically.” That is a crucial tell: if a rule makes the manga harder to draw *as manga*, the world often bends toward the comic’s readability.

   **Design consequence:** treat a Devil Fruit less like a spell list and more like a toy rule with infinite improv: one core premise plus (a) a weird visual edge case, (b) a comedic use, (c) a terrifying use, (d) a specific environmental counter, (e) a humiliating failure mode.

3. **Devil Fruit failure modes — Why fan-made fruits feel fake.**
   The most common failure is **overbreadth**: a fruit that does “everything” (multiple elements, multiple domains, universal telekinesis) removes the need for ingenuity and therefore removes the series’ primary fight pleasure: combat as a puzzle with body stakes and situational constraints.

   The second failure is **prestige seriousness**—powers that are too elegant, too edgy, too “cool to be stupid.” The author explicitly values having room to “fool around” even at maximum drama; if your power cannot generate humiliation and comedy *without breaking the plot*, it will read as generic anime superiority fantasy instead of One Piece.

   The third failure is **game-mechanical cleanliness**: cooldown meters, perfect counters, neat tiers, “balanced kits.” One Piece prefers messy constraints (sea weakness, seastone, morale breaks, weather, collateral, crowd pressure) and even builds in asymmetries like artificial-fruit edge cases that don’t behave like “normal” inheritance.

4. **Haki law — Haki is scene-direction, not a stat.**
   Haki is willpower/spiritual energy made usable. Mechanically it enables sensing/anticipation, invisible armor/offense, and (rarely) domination of others’ spirit; dramatically it decides who owns the room: who flinches, who can breathe, who dares to speak, who feels fate “closing in” before contact.

   Silvers Rayleigh matters here as a staging principle: the official anime synopsis frames his tutoring as “teaching Haki” on a harsh training island against huge beasts. Haki is not learned as a clean dojo montage; it is learned as survival pressure, predation, and repetition under risk.

   Observation Haki is explicitly tied to psychology. Charlotte Katakuri states that if an Observation user is not calm/collected, they cannot use it properly—so emotional destabilization becomes a real “counter,” and composure becomes a combat resource.

   Conqueror’s Haki is pure dominance writing: it asserts will over others. An SBS answer stresses that “how many people you knock out” depends on an overwhelming gap (not a neat ranking), and that advanced users can choose not to knock out specific people in a crowd—turning it into a crowd-scene tool, not a mere damage multiplier.

   Finally, Haki is allowed to feel “physical” when the story needs it to: an official Egghead-era column highlights the notion of storing Haki in a knot and releasing it later, reinforcing that the series treats “will” as something that can be staged in objects and environments, not just in auras.

5. **Physicality law — Bodies stay relevant because the world is violent and heavy.**
   Even with supernatural powers, bodies are machines that can be trained, broken, starved, drowned, and exhausted. This is why the story maintains parallel best‑in‑class “non-magic” systems: the Six Powers (Rokushiki) are described as simple maneuvers advanced to superhuman levels (speed steps, air walking, finger “pistols,” blade-like kicks, iron-hard defense, paper-like dodging), and they are strongly associated with government forces.

   CP9 embodies that design: trained from childhood into covert killers, with a style that screams “weaponized body.” Even where Devil Fruits and Haki exist, Rokushiki keeps fights grounded in impact timing, stamina, pain tolerance, and “how much body can you spend.”

   **Design consequence:** always budget body costs into fights: oxygen, grip, traction on unstable terrain, cumulative bruising, the physics of getting smashed through walls, and the omnipresent drowning risk that the official portal treats as the core Devil Fruit penalty in a sea world.

6. **Creativity and matchup law — A weaker power is dangerous when it has a sharp problem to solve.**
   The series repeatedly states and demonstrates that mastery and application matter as much as the “type” of power. Crocodile explicitly lectures that Devil Fruit abilities become formidable through training and proper use—framing fights as skill and application, not just possession.

   Early-era matchups establish that specificity beats grandeur: rubber’s insulating properties create immunity to electrical attacks, which means a lightning Logia’s “invincible element” becomes situational rather than absolute. Enel is forced into alternate tactics precisely because the obvious solution (lightning) fails against a rubber body.

   Comedy is often the cleanest matchup logic. Usopp survives a despair/negativity attack because he is “already negative,” making an oppressive psychological ability fail in a way that is humiliating and character-consistent. Perona is horrified not by being out-muscled but by discovering an immunity edge case.

   “Weird support” can also become lethal: Mr. 3 turns candle wax into steel-strong structures, keys, armor, and traps; the wax has clear weaknesses (heat), but within its domain it becomes a toolkit that solves prison problems, poison problems, and battlefield geometry problems.

   **Design consequence:** design fights as interaction tests—“does this power interact with that environment/psychology/body?” not “is A bigger than B?”

   Alabasta and Skypiea are especially instructive power-grammar arcs because their key conflicts revolve around environment logic (sand/water; lightning/rubber) and cultural physics (desert warfare; sky conditions), long before late-series Haki dominance becomes the default.

7. **Fighting style identity law — Silhouette first, rules second, gimmick last.**
   One Piece styles are recognizable at a glance because they have a consistent silhouette and repeatable rules: stance, weapon arrangement, thematic vocabulary, and a few signature “icon shots.” Roronoa Zoro is the archetype: “three-sword” is not just equipment, it is choreography and identity, which is why the style sustains dozens of named forms and variations without feeling like random move spam.

   Rokushiki is identity-by-function: each technique corresponds to a body stat pushed beyond human norms. Fish-Man Karate is identity-by-medium: it treats “water” as manipulable even when it’s not a convenient pool, turning environment into weapon logic.

   **Design consequence:** write styles as verbs with a camera: what does the body do, from what stance, at what rhythm, with what repeated “panel grammar”? Then let personality deform the style (sloppy, elegant, theatrical, cruel).

8. **Named attack law — Attack names are voice acting on the page.**
   Attack names work because they are character language: food puns, religious references, dumb onomatopoeia, absurd measurements, bilingual jokes, escalating suffixes. Sword-technique naming is famously pun-dense (including layered kanji readings and cultural references), which turns “naming a move” into part of the series’ playful texture rather than mere cataloging.

   The kick-fighter’s moveset ties heat and cuisine together, and even the “how does he not burn himself?” question gets an SBS-style character answer (his heart burns hotter). That is core One Piece logic: mechanics are often justified through emotion/comedy rather than hard simulation.

   **Design consequence:** treat naming as characterization. If you remove the names, the move list should still *sound* like that person. If names can be swapped between characters without anyone noticing, you are writing generic shonen move labels.

9. **Awakening law — Awakening is a worldview expansion with an identity risk.**
   Awakenings are not “Level 2.” They are rare, high-significance shifts that often change the *scope* of an ability rather than its “damage.” Donquixote Doflamingo is an explicit Paramecia example: awakening lets him transform nonliving surroundings into strings, turning the battlefield into his domain.

   Zoan awakenings carry a thematic warning label. In SBS commentary, the author confirms that the Zoan jailer beasts are cases where the user’s personality was absorbed by the awakened form—“Yes! That was close,” he quips about Rob Lucci, implying the risk is real even for elite fighters.

   Impel Down matters because awakenings are introduced through a carceral lens: awakened forms as dehumanized institutional beasts, a warning about power severed from selfhood.

   **Design consequence:** if you give an awakening, decide what it costs (identity drift, stamina debt, loss of control, collateral risk) and what it means (theme/ideology). Copying “environment control” without paying the thematic bill is the fastest way to make awakenings feel fake.

10. **Power-up law — New power lands when it is a solution to humiliation under pressure.**
   One Piece upgrades typically arrive at the intersection of (a) a hard loss or near-death humiliation, (b) a constraint that cannot be brute-forced, and (c) an emotional vow or value shift. The two-year training break is staged as explicit survival training: the official anime synopsis frames Haki education happening against giant beasts on a harsh island, and the official story recap frames the reunion afterward as a “complete revival” after two years.

   Late-series Haki upgrades are still written as *interaction rules*, not just “more aura.” In the Wano-era anime synopsis, Kaido is described clashing with Conqueror’s-coated force so intense that it produces non-contact deflection and shakes the fortress—an upgrade presented as “touching without touching,” not a flat stat increase.

   Rusukaina and Wano Country are useful bookends for pacing: one is “learn basics so you can survive,” the other is “refine basics into elite applications under war pressure.”

   **Design consequence:** a power-up should feel like the character discovered a new sentence they can speak in the existing language, not like the author added a new system.

11. **Long-form progression law — Scale by problems, not numbers.**
   The official story index shows the series blocks its long journey into arcs with different constraints (desert war, sky survival, judicial island raid, prison escape, public war, tyranny puzzle, closed-nation siege, science conflict). Each arc introduces new power questions without invalidating earlier grammar.

   The series also keeps “early-era excitement” alive by continuing to stage non-top-tier conflicts inside top-tier arcs. For example, a Wano raid episode synopsis shows that while the top clash shakes the island, other crew members are still running from stronger opponents, protecting civilians, and improvising under fear—tension maintained through vulnerability and local objectives even when god-tier fights exist elsewhere.

   **Design consequence:** keep multiple power “tiers” active simultaneously via parallel objectives (escape routes, hostage protection, countdown devices, perception battles, sabotage). Don’t let the “strongest clash” monopolize the entire arc’s emotional bandwidth.

12. **Rival and villain power law — Enemies turn power into a regime.**
   Villains rarely use power only to win a duel; they use it to govern—cages, monopolies, executions, forced choices. The official story recap of the great public war frames a state-planned execution as an attempt to “cut off” a bloodline, prompting a world-shaking battle in response.

   Labels matter here: World Government and Marines are not background factions; they are institutions that weaponize spectacle, law, secret policing, and military logistics as power.

   Seven Warlords of the Sea embody “power outsourced”: criminals granted legitimacy to enforce order through terror, blurring the line between state strength and pirate strength.

   In that war, Portgas D. Ace becomes symbol rather than soldier—execution as history-writing. And the quake power’s reputation becomes a political object the moment it is stolen: Whitebeard dies and Blackbeard takes the ability, turning the battlefield into a global shock event.

   **Design consequence:** give villains regime moves, not just finishers: time limits, hostage geometry, omnipresent surveillance, forced-choice dilemmas, public humiliation engines.

13. **Crew role balance law — Equality of importance, not equality of damage.**
   The official portal frames Straw Hat Pirates as a crew defined by roles, dreams, and a shared journey structure (formed in the East, reunites after two years, advances into the New World toward the final island). That framing is crucial: “crew power” is not just stacking damage; it is stacking capabilities that let the crew exist (navigation, medicine, intel, craftsmanship, morale).

   Nami is described on the official site as the navigator with exceptional ability to read weather; “without her, the crew’s voyage can’t function.” That is a power statement even before combat: navigation competence is treated as life-or-death power in a sea world.

   **Design consequence:** do not make everyone chase the same combat ceiling. Give each crew member a domain of necessity (the plot literally cannot progress without them), and give them a combat style that expresses that domain (weather as weapon, medicine as battlefield triage, music as morale/tempo, etc.).

14. **Public spectacle and aura law — Power feels huge when other people have to live with it.**
   One Piece makes top-tier power feel enormous by staging it in public contexts with collateral and witnesses. The official story recap of Marineford frames the conflict as an unprecedented battle whose outcome would “change the era,” with mass forces, betrayals, and world-level threats—absorbed by the wider world through myth and headlines.

   Likewise, Dressrosa is framed as spectacle built on crowd mechanics: a colosseum tournament, a city-wide “birdcage” countdown that forces panic, and a final confrontation where the battlefield becomes part of the tyrant’s control.

   Conqueror’s Haki is the cleanest “aura without empty hype” tool because it has immediate crowd readability. The SBS note on knockouts emphasizes it depends on an overwhelming gap and can be selectively controlled—so it can signal dominance to a crowd without turning everyone nearby into collateral.

   **Design consequence:** to create aura, write the reaction chain: silence → posture collapse → environmental response (cracks, swaying, weather shift) → crowd decision (run, kneel, cheer, pray, riot). If nobody reacts, the power won’t feel big.

15. **System-level failure modes — What destroys tension in long-form One Piece-style power.**
   The fastest way to turn your saga into sludge is to treat systems as clean and purely competitive: perfect counters, universal defenses, neat tier ceilings, awakenings-on-demand. Canon resists this: Devil Fruits have hard world penalties; Haki has psychological conditions; awakenings can threaten identity; arc structure keeps injecting constraints rather than only escalating numbers.

   The second fastest way is deleting humor and humiliation. The SBS philosophy behind the hero’s ridiculousness is explicitly about preserving room to “fool around” even at maximum seriousness; without that, late-game power becomes sterile and self-important.

   The third fastest way is forgetting politics and public life. Official recaps of major arcs show fights embedded in institutions (executions, coups, news, armies, slavery). If your strongest characters only fight in empty arenas, you lose the “world shaking” feel that One Piece gets by making power a public event.

## Rulebooks and design laws for an original saga

**A) One Piece power grammar rulebook**
These are not generic magic-system tips; they are the repeated *canon behaviors* that keep the series exciting without collapsing into tier lists.

Rule: **Keep powers singular, make applications infinite.** Pick one core premise per power kit; let creativity come from interaction, not additional domains.

Rule: **Every kit needs an embarrassment mode.** If the user cannot be made ridiculous by their own ability (or by the world’s reaction), the power drifts into generic superiority fantasy.

Rule: **Every kit needs an oppression mode.** Even silly powers should have a terrifying application when combined with cruelty, resources, or institutions.

Rule: **Haki is social dominance before it is “armor.”** Use it to shift intimidation, initiative, and crowd behavior before using it as hardness. Psychological counters (fear, anger, distraction) should matter as much as physical counters.

Rule: **Bodies matter.** Track stamina, breath, traction, pain, and drowning risk. Don’t let powers erase the sea’s hostility.

Rule: **Matchups are authored, not computed.** Build interactions that reveal character (negativity immunity; rubber insulation; wax keys). Avoid “my aura is bigger” solutions unless the scene is explicitly about domination and fear.

Rule: **Awakenings are thematic contracts.** Tie them to identity risk or ideological climax; do not hand them out as routine escalation.

Rule: **Progression is island-shaped.** Each arc should introduce a new constraint that forces new applications of old tools; don’t just scale opponent stats.

Rule: **Villains build regimes.** Give antagonists regime tools—cages, laws, armies, propaganda, hostage logic—so defeating them feels like liberating a world, not winning a match.

Rule: **Crew balance is narrative necessity.** Make each member indispensable; let combat competence express job and worldview rather than chasing identical ceilings.

Rule: **Spectacle needs witnesses.** If the world doesn’t react, the power doesn’t land. Build reaction chains and public stakes.

**B) Devil Fruit creation law**
A One Piece-native Devil Fruit usually has:

A **simple core**: one noun/verb premise explainable in one sentence. The official taxonomy encourages that: Paramecia (broad “superhuman”), Zoan (animal transformation), Logia (natural element transformation).

A **body-anchored expression**: the power changes body logic or sensory logic in a way you can draw repeatedly (stretch, split, bloom, ooze, harden). The series explicitly allows “comic pragmatism” (like clothing logic) to keep powers readable as manga.

A **comedy-to-horror slider**: one use case that’s funny in low stakes and horrifying with malice/resources.

At least **two crisp weaknesses**: one systemic (sea, seastone, stamina debt; sometimes heat/cold) and one situational (material, mental state, terrain).

A **signature humiliation**: a way the power backfires or forces an undignified posture. This is a major “anti‑AI” filter because it demands taste, not optimization.

A **signature terror**: a way the same premise becomes oppressive in an antagonist’s hands.

A **progression ladder that is not a new domain**: the same premise refined into control → speed → coverage → finesse → (maybe) awakening only if story/theme demands it.

Quick fake-check: if your fruit description needs a paragraph of “also it can…” to feel cool, it’s already drifting away from the canon’s power grammar.

**C) Haki, growth, and power-up law**
Write growth as shifts in *how a character applies pressure*, not as a meter.

Observation growth: improve information quality (range, precision, emotional reading, future glimpses) and remember it is tied to mental state; destabilization is a valid counter.

Armament growth: improve coverage, timing, and emission; treat it as technique married to conditioning, not a permanent “always on” buff.

Conqueror’s growth: treat it as dominance writing. The SBS note says knockout scale depends on the gap and that advanced control can spare targets in a crowd; use that as a tool for leadership, intimidation, and who owns the space.

Power-up landing checklist (canon-consistent):

- **Humiliation first**: the character learns their old idea fails.
- **Constraint next**: drowning risk, time limit, hostage geometry, mental destabilization.
- **Vow/value shift**: protect someone, refuse to yield, reclaim identity.
- **New sentence, not new language**: new application of existing grammar.

If you skip straight to “new form” without humiliation/constraint, you get cheapness: the audience feels the author’s hand instead of the character’s will.

**D) Attack name and moveset law**
Build move names from:

- **Personal lexicon**: cuisine for chefs, religious references for ascetic swordsmen, childish onomatopoeia for tricksters.
- **Escalation through exaggeration**: bigger numbers, longer titles, compounded puns—while keeping the base phrase recognizable.
- **Status shifts**: when worldview changes, move naming can shift (more vicious, more noble, more childish, more controlled).

A native moveset also includes **non-lethal utility verbs** (mobility, restraint, deception) and **crowd-safe verbs** (selective intimidation, targeted knockouts), because fights often occur around civilians and allies.

## Practical power design template

**E) A usable One Piece power kit template**
Use this as a worksheet for any major fighter (hero, rival, villain, specialist).

**Body base**
- Species/body type (human, giant, fish-man, cyborg, etc.)
- Physical truth: what their body is built for (endurance, speed, reach, mass, flexibility).

**Fighting style**
- Signature silhouette (stance/weapon arrangement)
- Three core verbs (slash / grab / explode, etc.)
- One non-negotiable rule (ethical or professional constraint).

**Devil Fruit**
- One-sentence premise
- Three applications: comedy / utility / horror
- Two crisp weaknesses: systemic + situational
- How sea weakness shapes behavior (ships, rescue plans, arrogance risk).

**Haki profile**
- Observation: what do they read (movement, intent, emotions)? What mental state breaks it?
- Armament: where do they coat, how often, what is the stamina cost?
- Conqueror’s: if yes, what does it *say* about them? Can they control collateral in crowds?

**Strengths and weaknesses**
- Strengths are situations they solve
- Weaknesses are situations that make them small.

**Environmental interaction**
- Favorite terrain (tight corridors, open decks, storms, crowds)
- Natural counters (heat, water, mirrors, sand, metal, etc.).

**Emotional trigger points**
- What makes them lose composure (and therefore lose Observation or make bad choices)?
- What makes them lock in (protecting someone, humiliation, inherited promise)?

**Likely progression path**
- Phase 1: learn the one-verb premise
- Phase 2: master control + utility
- Phase 3: develop one signature interaction counter
- Phase 4: combine with Haki or refine technique
- Phase 5: awakening only if identity/theme demands it, with explicit cost.

**Failure risks**
- Overbreadth (too many domains)
- No humiliation mode
- No social/public consequences
- Awakening becomes routine.

### C. Haki tier ladder (operational)

The full operational module lives at `_reference/haki-tiers.md`. The summary here:

**Observation (Kenbunshoku)**
- Basic: sense intent, presence, count people in next room. Most-trained-fighter baseline.
- Advanced: Future Sight — see opponent's next move as a flicker before it lands. Katakuri, Luffy post-Whole-Cake. **Counter: emotional destabilization** (Katakuri loses Future Sight when shaken).

**Armament (Busoshoku)**
- Basic: invisible coating; turns body parts into "iron." Touch a Logia. Block named attacks.
- Advanced (Internal Destruction / Ryuou): bypass armor, damage internal organs without breaking skin. Vivi's father, Rayleigh, Luffy post-skip.
- Color-bearing (Black Armament): visible black coating; signals top-tier. Required to scratch a Yonko.

**Conqueror's (Haoshoku)**
- Basic: knock out the weak-willed in a crowd. Roughly 1 in millions has it. Selectively — sparing chosen targets — is intermediate skill.
- Advanced (Coating): coat strikes with black lightning; damage WITHOUT TOUCHING. Reserved for Yonko-tier and a few rising rookies. Luffy, Kaido, Big Mom, Shanks, Roger, Whitebeard, Rayleigh.
- **Conqueror's clash**: when two Conqueror's-coated strikes collide, the SKY SPLITS. Visible to the entire island. Roger vs Whitebeard, Luffy vs Kaido.

### D. Devil Fruit awakening rules

The full operational module lives at `_reference/devil-fruit-awakening.md`. The summary here:

**Paramecia awakening**: turn the WORLD into the medium of your power. Doflamingo's strings become buildings. Katakuri's mochi becomes the floor. Law's Room becomes ambient. **Cost**: massive stamina drain, identity-dependent expression. **Trigger**: extreme duress + decades of mastery + ideological commitment.

**Zoan awakening**: enhanced regen, stamina, healing, durability — physical limits expand. **Identity risk**: the animal personality can absorb the human (Impel Down jailer beasts). Mythical Zoans awaken with thematic-mythic side-effects (Marco's blue flames, Sengoku's Buddha shockwaves, Yamato's wolf-ice).

**Logia awakening**: Canon-vague. Implied by Blackbeard's quake-darkness duality and World Gov fear of unstable Logia awakening. Likely: convert area into your element on a permanent or semi-permanent basis. Treat as **saga-rare** — at most one per major arc, never as routine escalation.

**Artificial fruit (SMILE) awakening**: doesn't apply — SMILE users are stuck at failed-Zoan. Awakening is for natural fruits.

### E. Sea Prism Stone (Kairouseki)

**What it is**: dense oceanic mineral that emits the energy of "the sea itself." Devil Fruit users in skin contact with it lose their powers AND become weak — sometimes worse than seawater submersion (drains stamina actively, not passively).

**Forms in canon use**:
- Marine handcuffs (standard issue for any DF prisoner)
- Impel Down cell bars and floors
- Bullets with seastone cores (rare; fired by elite Marines and bounty hunters)
- Fishing nets to capture DF-using sea predators
- Coating on warship hulls (some flagships) to hide presence from sea-sensitive creatures
- Personal weapons (Smoker's jitte, Vergo's bamboo staff)

**Counters / resistances**:
- **No skin contact** = no effect. Cloth, gloves, coats can buffer.
- **Strong-willed users** can move and fight while restrained, just at reduced power (Luffy in Impel Down).
- **Logia**: completely depowered on contact. Turns Aokiji into a wet civilian.
- **Zoan**: locked into base form, no transformation, no enhanced strength.
- **Paramecia**: power off; some constraints (e.g. Robin can still PUNCH, just no flowers).

**Rarity / economics**: Government-controlled mining. Black market price absurd — a single bullet is contraband. NOT something every Marine carries.

### F. Yonko / Admiral threshold (saga ceiling)

**Yonko (Four Emperors of the Sea)** — at any time, the four most powerful pirate captains in the New World. Status is **reputation + force projection**, not a council seat. Becoming a Yonko requires:
1. Holding territory in the New World (allied islands, protected populations)
2. A bounty in the 1.5B+ band
3. Conqueror's Haki at advanced level (Coating)
4. The world EXPECTING you — gossip, headlines, fear among other crews
5. Surviving the response from the OTHER three and the Marines

**Admiral threshold (Marine equivalent)**: equivalent in raw power. Logia-tier or Mythical Zoan or Paramecia of overwhelming domain. Garp refused promotion to Admiral; that refusal is canon character note — strongest VA in history because he chose to stay there.

**Yonko vs Admiral**: rough parity. Marineford set the precedent — a coordinated Admiral attack force can wound Whitebeard, but it cost lives and a Warlord betrayal to land it.

**For the Merdo saga**: Yonko / Admiral tier is the SAGA CEILING. Cameos only until late saga. A Yonko notices Merdo only after a public spectacle. An Admiral arrives only when the World Government decides he is a structural threat. Until then, the antagonist ladder caps at Vice-Admiral / Yonko-commander.

### H. Power-call quick lookup (during play)

When a fight call needs a snap judgment:

1. **Who has Conqueror's coating?** Check Section C. If neither side does, no skies split, no non-contact damage.
2. **Is the matchup a Logia vs non-Haki user?** Section A reads — without Haki, you can't touch a Logia. Story finds a workaround (rubber, water, environment).
3. **Bounty mismatch read?** Section A — a 30M rookie LOSES against a 300M target unless the fight has a specific puzzle key.
4. **Marine rank?** Section B — a Captain leading a warship is the local tier; the Vice-Admiral is the regional tier; the Admiral is the world-tier event.
5. **Devil Fruit awakening on the table?** Section D — only under the strict triggers; never routine.
6. **Sea Prism Stone in play?** Section E — instantly changes the geometry. Any DF user is now relying only on body and skill.
