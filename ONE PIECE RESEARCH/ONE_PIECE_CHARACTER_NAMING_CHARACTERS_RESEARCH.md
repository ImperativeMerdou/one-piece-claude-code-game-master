# One Piece Character & Naming Grammar Extraction

## Research basis and what “belongs” means here

This analysis treats “feels like One Piece” as a **recognition problem**: a character must be identifiable at speed (weekly serialization + dense panels), legible from a distance, quotable without audio, and emotionally “readable” within a few beats—while still leaving room for surprise.

Primary scaffolding used in this extraction:

- **Creator statements about design priorities**: Eiichiro Oda explicitly prioritizes *character models* and the joy of inventing new silhouettes, and he notes that he makes episodes to use those silhouettes quickly.
- **Creator statements about contradiction and dichotomy**: he explicitly calls out the “funny dichotomy” between appearance and actions (e.g., characters like Emporio Ivankov and Bentham), and describes his affection for characters who are “annoying yet cunning” (e.g., Buggy).
- **In-world “UI” sources and official profiles**: the official character database (nicknames/epithets, short profiles, dreams, verbal tics/catchphrases) is invaluable because it shows what the franchise itself considers the character’s *headline identity*.
- **Official reference infrastructure**: Shueisha describes the VIVRE CARD character reference as a binder-style fanbook where each card executes “one character per card,” with profiles, hidden facets, extras, and chronology—i.e., *character as a compact data object*.

When I say “design grammar,” I mean **repeatable production rules** that consistently generate “instant icons” within this aesthetic system—not generic character advice. The output is built as a **toolkit**: constraints, tests, and failure conditions.

## The core grammar that makes a character instantly One Piece

### Name grammar as a micro-story, not a label

A One Piece name usually does at least **two** of these jobs at once:

**Phonetic hook + social function.** Names “stick” because they have a mouthfeel (rhythm, consonant punch, repetition) and also function socially: they’re easy for other characters to shout, shorten, mishear, or nickname. This is why nicknames/通称 and in-world “handles” are structurally important: Smoker is “White Hunter,” but subordinates call him “Smoyan,” a built-in intimacy lever.

**Mixed-register friction.** One Piece loves stacking “grand” and “stupid” in the same naming space. The official profile for Buggy explicitly bakes in both his clown framing and his elevated bounty-era status, while also keeping the petty insecurity (his nose).

**Borrowed reality + distortion.** Oda has stated that some names are taken from real pirates (he cites Roronoa Zoro as being based on “François l’Olonnais”). This matters because it’s not generic fantasy phonology; it’s **real-world salt** filtered through manga stylization.

**Codename systems that are playful, not sterile.** Government/spy structures (CP) and criminal orgs (Baroque Works) use systematic naming (numbers, titles, paired code formats), but One Piece makes the system itself a *joke engine* and a *power readability engine*. Official profiles preserve these structures (Mr.1, Mr.5, etc.), turning what could be generic espionage into something toyetic and legible.

**Key test:** If your name cannot be plausibly printed in a newspaper headline, shouted across a battlefield, and turned into a mocking nickname by Monkey D. Luffy in the same chapter, it’s not yet One Piece enough. (This is an inference from how the series operationalizes epithets, Wanted-style UI, and nickname play.)

### Silhouette grammar as the “first read” contract

Oda explicitly frames silhouette invention as a peak pleasure: creating a silhouette “no one’s ever seen before” and then building episodes to deploy it.  That one statement explains a lot of why the cast feels instantly iconic.

Silhouette in this system is not “cool design.” It’s **information architecture**:

- **One dominant shape anchor** (circle/box/triangle/coil/column) that reads in black-fill.
- **One “body logic” absurdity** that is still consistent (cyborg modularity, giant proportions, animal-human fusion, etc.).
- **One prop/garment that is inseparable from the outline** (hat, coat drape, weapon, hairstyle, mask).

The official database repeatedly foregrounds these anchors as identity: Luffy’s public identity is “Straw Hat” (hat silhouette as torso symbol).  Franky is “Iron Man” and his page leans hard on body modification and built-in gimmicks (even “mostly meaningless” funny functions), which is silhouette logic turned into personality logic.

The franchise also uses silhouette as **tease and anticipation tech**—even in official commentary describing how silhouettes are used to hint at incoming threats.

### Character compression as “one emotional truth” you can feel fast

One Piece character compression is not about reducing complexity; it’s about **front-loading a single emotional thesis** that the character never stops expressing—even while the plot adds layers.

The official character encyclopedia pages often encode this thesis in one sentence:

- Dr. Hiriluk: “There is no incurable disease” energy—straight belief as identity.
- Bellemere: chosen parenthood + defiant protection (she sacrifices herself to protect her daughters).
- Laboon: waiting as devotion—50+ years of belief in a promise.

Compression works here because it makes the character **scene-ready**: you can drop them into any moment and know what they’ll do under pressure.

### Contradiction as the life-giver, not a “twist”

Oda himself points at a central engine: the “funny dichotomy between actions and appearance” makes him want to draw certain characters more.  This is not optional; it’s the core anti-archetype rule.

In One Piece, contradiction tends to be **visible** and **repeatable**:

- Borsalino: “casual tone” vs “merciless attacks” is explicitly stated in his official profile—contradiction is part of the brand.
- Vergo: trusted Marine leader + undercover pirate spy; plus “airhead” gag (food stuck on face) embedded alongside espionage cruelty.
- Senor Pink: baby costume silhouette + hardboiled diction and fan adoration—this is contradiction weaponized into instant icon status.

The key is that the contradiction is not a late reveal. It’s usually present on the first read.

### Comedy engine as a repeating machine that connects to the wound

One Piece comedy is often **mechanical**: a behavior loop repeats, escalates, and becomes the character’s “sound” in the story. But it’s rarely disconnected from the emotional core.

Examples where official profiles show the loop directly:

- Franky: openly “hardboiled/pervert” duality, plus a literal catchphrase (“Super!!”)—a repeatable button you can press in any scene.
- Kureha: “What’s the secret of youth?” as a verbal loop that signals personality before plot.
- Pagaya: habitual apology (“sorry”) as a social comedy engine.
- Buggy: pride wound (nose sensitivity) is the comedy trigger, while his “cunning/annoying” duality keeps him threatening enough to matter.

Structural note: Oda explicitly prefers post-battle feasts and avoids death heaviness because it would poison the party tone; this is an authorial rule that keeps comedy and catharsis fused rather than segregated.

### Speech flavor as audible identity in a silent medium

One Piece dialogue identity is built from:

- **Register choice** (formal vs slang vs performative)
- **Sentence length + rhythm** (fast staccato vs slow grand proclamations)
- **Verbal tics/catchphrases** (repeatable)
- **Nicknaming behavior** (who they rename and how)
- **Distinct laughter styles** (a textual audio cue)

The world even codifies “distinctive laughter styles” as a widespread trait (Warai-kata), making laugh-writing a core identifiability tool.

Oda also describes his pages as crowded with “sound” and insists on drawing everything that “moves” to keep that living density.  That sensibility pushes characters toward **highly textured verbal and behavioral signals**—they have to be “heard” on the page.

### Dream, wound, and forward pull as a vector

A “One Piece” character nearly always has a **vector**: they’re pointed at a future noun phrase, even if they’re morally rotten. This is not generic “motivation”; it’s a **forward pull that can be named**.

Official profiles make this explicit for many core characters:

- Nico Robin: dream is to see the Rio Poneglyph / “true history,” and her wound is being hunted as “Devil Child.”
- Tony Tony Chopper: dream is to become a “panacea” (a doctor who can cure anything), rooted in the trauma of being treated as a monster and saved by Hiriluk’s belief.
- Franky: dream is to sail on his “dream ship” to the “end of the sea,” welded to his identity as the builder of that ship.
- Brook: forward pull is reunion with Laboon after decades of isolation—promise + time as emotional fuel.

For villains/tyrants, the “dream” is often **control-shaped** (world destruction, domination, weapon markets), but it still acts as a vector. Donquixote Doflamingo explicitly wants to destroy the world rule of the Celestial Dragons due to his upbringing, and he operates as an underworld arms broker.

### The lovable / annoying / dangerous tri-balance

One Piece doesn’t merely “blend tones.” It engineers a **three-knob balance**:

- **Lovable** = a humanizing sincerity or loyalty cue
- **Annoying** = a repeatable flaw that forces friction
- **Dangerous** = competence or threat projection that still holds

Buggy is a canonical demonstration: Oda highlights him as “annoying yet cunning,” and the official profile keeps both the clown identity and the volatile pride trigger intact.
Smoker is another: principled, disobedient to upper ranks, but respected and nicknamed by subordinates—human, irritating to bureaucracy, still a serious pursuer.

### Iconic prop/body logic as memory lock

One Piece often makes the “remember me” asset a **physical rule**:

- Brook’s body logic: revived soul → skeleton musician (silhouette + concept locked).
- Rob Lucci’s constant pigeon companion (Hattori) is explicitly noted in the official profile—prop as identity stamp.
- Duval’s mask/face + wanted-poster gag becomes his entire “why he exists” logic.

The key difference from generic character design: the prop is not “cool.” It is a **story machine** (it causes misunderstandings, triggers loyalty tests, enables reveals, or forces scenes to happen).

### Titles, epithets, and bounty-language as diegetic branding

One Piece treats epithets (通称) like **in-world product names**:

- “Straw Hat” is literally a wearable silhouette icon turned into public identity.
- “Devil Child” is social persecution compressed into two words.
- “White Hunter” is profession + visual (smoke/white) fused into a chase brand.
- Beast Pirates’ top officers use disaster epithets (“Wildfire,” “Drought”), compressing threat category into a single headline noun.

The bounty system itself has built-in misreporting and misconception mechanics (e.g., bounties being too low/high due to secrecy, bribery, or misunderstanding), which is crucial: it makes “iconic presence” partly a **media artifact** inside the world, not just a power level.

A related SBS principle matters for villain design: Oda explicitly says that a villain feeling “sketchy” can increase mystery/charm, and that “good” villains are hard to make. This is a direct warning against over-explaining your tyrant too early.

## Category grammars and how construction shifts by role

Below are the *differences in naming + construction* by archetype class—what changes, what stays constant, and what tends to be overcopied badly.

### Pirate captains

Pirate captains tend to have **headline-ready public handles** plus a silhouette that reads like a flag. The official database shows this as “通称 + bounty + fruit + role,” meaning captains are introduced as *brand objects*.

- **Name grammar:** often a big, clean hook (short or strongly segmented), frequently paired with an epithet that turns them into a newspaper object (e.g., “Straw Hat,” “Beasts”).
- **Silhouette grammar:** one dominant shape idea (dragon mass, clown ornamentation, hat/coat) that functions even in a crowd scene.
- **Contradiction:** captains often contain a strong “public myth vs private flaw” hinge (Buggy’s insecurity and luck-based mythmaking is explicitly supported via Oda’s own remarks and the bounty misconception structure).
- **Titles:** usually earned by what the world notices first (silhouette feature, “disaster” category, or public act).

**Overcopy pitfall:** making your captain’s name “cool fantasy sludge” (Latinate, overlong, no nickname potential) and then stapling “the ____” epithet that no one in-world would actually say.

### First mates and “right-hand” rivals

Right-hand figures tend to be defined less by big public headlines and more by **relationship function**: they are a stabilizer, counterweight, or blade. The “public brand” exists, but the *in-scene engine* is often loyalty + competence.

Oda’s naming confession about Zoro’s real-pirate inspiration signals that even this role still benefits from “real-world salt,” not pure fantasy naming.

**Overcopy pitfall:** making the first mate “edgy cool guy” without a repeatable comedy/annoyance knob. In One Piece, even hyper-competent characters are rarely allowed to be frictionless in every room (this is consistent with Oda’s taste for dichotomy).

### Comic crew members

Comic crew members are often built as **behavior machines** with deep emotional cores. Their construction leans hard on:

- **Catchphrase + posture + prop** (Franky’s “Super!!” + body gimmicks; Brook’s skeleton musician identity; Chopper’s cuteness vs medical seriousness).
- **Dream stated cleanly** (panacea doctor; reach the end with my dream ship; reunion promise).
- **Bounty/epithet as gag amplifier** (Chopper’s tiny bounty; Usopp’s “Sogeking” bounty identity).

**Overcopy pitfall:** giving your comic crew member “random quirky trait” that does not connect to a wound, a job aboard ship, or a repeatable interaction loop.

### Marines

Marines are a uniformed faction, so their silhouette grammar uses **controlled variation**: recognizable “Marine-ness” + one subversion element.

- Smoker: “White Hunter,” smoke body logic, outsider-in-the-institution vibe, and even an internal nickname used by subordinates (“Smoyan”).
- Garp as “legendary Marine hero” is described in official terms, but his core drama is explicitly framed as duty vs family conflict.
- Borsalino is an explicit contradiction spec: casual speech register paired with ruthless action.

**Overcopy pitfall:** writing Marines as either purely noble knights or purely fascist monsters. One Piece tends to generate scene-life through internal contradiction (principle vs orders, family vs duty, tone vs violence).

### Cipher Pol and government killers

Cipher Pol identity is “bureaucratic stealth + theatrical menace.” Construction leans on:

- **Codified organizational naming** (CP0 / “Aigis,” etc.) that still feels toyetic.
- **Accessory logic**: Rob Lucci’s pigeon is literally written into his official profile because it provides a stable silhouette/identity tag.
- **Undercover role-play**: CP9-era infiltration is part of the identity.

**Overcopy pitfall:** making them “cool assassins” without the administrative absurdity (paperwork vibe, code labels, petty careerism like Spandam’s nepotism/ambition).

### Tyrants and regime villains

Tyrant villains are often constructed as **systems**, not just fighters: they are kings, brokers, wardens, or governors, and their iconography spreads into the environment.

- Doflamingo’s official profile frames him as Dressrosa’s king + warlord + underworld arms broker; the villain concept is “power as market + power as theater.”
- Kaido’s official profile compresses him as “Beasts” + dragon transformation + rule over Wano with an ally tyrant; again, villain as regime.
- Oda’s SBS remark warns that “sketchy” villains gain mystery/charm—tyrant construction often benefits from not fully clarifying motive too early.

**Overcopy pitfall:** “sympathetic villain monologue” too early, or a tyrant whose evil is only personal, not infrastructural. One Piece tyrants are frequently felt through *what they do to the island* (law, markets, slavery, manufacturing), not only through personal cruelty.

### Tragic side characters and emotionally devastating allies

Tragic side characters are compressed so hard they become **moral accelerants** for the main cast.

- Bellemere’s official summary is essentially a one-sentence tragedy engine: adoptive mother, village occupied, dies to protect daughters.
- Hiriluk is belief-as-identity; his philosophy becomes Chopper’s forward pull.
- Koala’s official profile explicitly describes emotional suppression due to slavery and regaining a smile—this is “wound → visible behavioral change,” not abstract sadness.

**Overcopy pitfall:** writing tragedy as only “dark backstory.” In One Piece, the tragedy usually crystallizes into a *simple vow*, a *symbol*, or a *behavioral shift* that’s easy to carry forward on stage.

### Island weirdos and civilians

Island weirdos are not filler; they are **tone and theme amplifiers**. They tend to be:

- **High-concept gag silhouettes** that are still emotionally legible.
  - Gaimon: man stuck in a treasure chest for 20 years—absurd silhouette, but instantly understandable emotional thesis (waiting/guarding).
  - Duval: life ruined by a bad wanted sketch; becomes loyal after “face fix” — comedy built from bureaucracy/media artifacts.
- **Speech tic civilians**: Pagaya’s habitual apology (“sorry”) is a tiny loop that gives him instant presence.

**Overcopy pitfall:** weirdness with no emotional compression. One Piece weirdos are usually weird in shape but clean in heart/need.

### Underworld figures and media power

Underworld figures are “villains” who often fight with **information and reputation**, not fists.

- Morgans is explicitly “Big News,” president of the World Economy Newspaper, and also one of the “emperors of the underworld.” That is pure One Piece: media boss as underworld king, with a bird-body fruit for silhouette unity.

**Overcopy pitfall:** making underworld figures “generic crime lords.” The One Piece flavor is when the underworld has a *theatrical job title* (news, shipping, entertainment, auction houses) that doubles as a plot distribution network.

### Monsters, beasts, and the “still human” rule

Nonhuman entities feel One Piece when they still carry:

- **A vow/promise** (Laboon waiting)
- **A relationship-shaped wound** (Brook’s promise and decades alone)
- **A simple readable want** (food, home, being seen as a person—Chopper’s root wound)

**Overcopy pitfall:** “cool monster design” without a relational vector.

### Children

Children in One Piece frequently function as **emotional truth amplifiers**: a small body holds a giant belief or reveals a society’s cruelty. Even a tiny civilian like Tamachibi is framed as a pure gratitude signal amid adult cynicism, a micro-scene tool.

**Overcopy pitfall:** children written as either mascots or trauma devices only. One Piece often gives them an *active emotional function* in scenes (forgiving, insisting, admiring, exposing hypocrisy).

## Failure modes that create “generic anime sludge” instead of One Piece

These are the most common “fake-ness generators,” mapped to the grammar above.

**Silhouette failure:** “Human-shaped cool person with cool clothes.” If the character cannot be recognized in black-fill in under two seconds, you’ve broken the silhouette priority Oda explicitly values.

**Name failure:** high-fantasy Latinate strings with no pun, no mouthfeel, no nickname elasticity, no in-world headline function. Oda explicitly treats naming as important enough that he answers questions about origins and pulls from real pirates; One Piece names are rarely “neutral.”

**Contradiction failure:** “single-note badass” or “single-note cinnamon roll.” Oda directly expresses love for dichotomy (appearance vs actions) and for characters who are annoying yet cunning; flattening removes scene-life.

**Comedy failure:** quirks that do not repeat, do not escalate, and do not connect to a wound or social friction. One Piece comedy is often an engine (catchphrase loops, identity misunderstandings, petty insecurities) that can run forever.

**Dream/wound failure:** vague aspirations (“I want freedom,” “I want revenge”) with no physicalizable noun phrase, or tragedy with no carry-forward vow. Official profiles repeatedly compress dreams into concrete targets (panacea, true history, end of the sea, reunion promise).

**Title/epithet failure:** self-proclaimed “The Dark ____” with no in-world adoption, no media system, no misinterpretation mechanism. In One Piece, epithets function as public branding and often arise from what outsiders notice (silhouette, behavior, incidents, misconceptions).

**Overcopied fan/AI tells (high-risk fake signals):**
- Copying surface aesthetics (big coat, scar, cigar) without the three-knob balance (lovable/annoying/dangerous).
- Producing “Oda-style weird” that is only grotesque. Oda’s weirdness usually remains readable and emotionally compressible (Gaimon, Duval).
- Making government/CP characters purely sleek. CP characters often include petty careerism or absurd tags (Spandam’s climb; Lucci’s pigeon; codename structures).

## One Piece character construction rulebook

Below is a production rulebook written as “laws.” Each law includes what it controls and how to test it. (These laws are derived from the cited creator statements + how the official profiles and in-world UI encode identity.)

**Silhouette law**
A character must have one primary shape idea and one inseparable prop/garment that makes them readable in black-fill. If you can remove the prop and nothing changes, the prop is decorative, not iconic. Oda explicitly chases novel silhouettes and builds episodes to deploy them.

**Contradiction law**
Every major character needs an on-stage contradiction that can be demonstrated in a single panel/page (appearance vs behavior, tone vs violence, loyalty vs cruelty). Oda explicitly says the dichotomy is what makes some characters fun to draw, and official profiles bake contradictions into identity text (e.g., casual tone vs merciless attack).

**Comedy engine law**
Comedy must be mechanized: a repeatable loop (phrase, insecurity trigger, misunderstanding, habitual behavior) that can fire in any scene—and it should connect to a wound or social friction. Franky’s catchphrase and “pervert but loyal” identity is the template for a comedy engine that also has heart.

**Speech law**
Give the character a text-audio identity: register + rhythm + tic. Distinctive laughs are an established world trait, and catchphrases are repeatedly used in official summaries.

**Dream law**
A One Piece dream should be a *noun phrase you can point at*: “panacea,” “true history,” “end of the sea,” “reunion,” “King of the Pirates.” If the dream can’t fit in a headline, it won’t pull. This is consistent with how the official database describes character dreams.

**Wound law**
The wound must be **portable**: it should change how the character behaves in ordinary moments, not only in flashback. Koala’s emotional suppression and regained smile is described as behavioral consequence, not abstract trauma.

**Quirk law**
Quirks must be either a) a social interaction trap (forces others to respond) or b) a plot lever (causes events). Duval’s entire existence is a bureaucracy/media trap; it creates story because it’s tied to a wanted poster error.

**Memorability law**
One clean hook per channel: one silhouette hook, one name hook, one voice hook, one emotional truth. Do not spread uniqueness across ten subtle details; concentrate it. This matches the “one character per card” mentality of official reference products and the official database’s “通称 + essence” compression.

**Lovable/annoying/dangerous law**
Set all three knobs deliberately. If you set only lovable+dangrous you get bland competence; lovable+annoying without danger becomes disposable gag; annoying+dangerous without lovable becomes flat villain. Oda’s stated affection for “annoying yet cunning” is basically this law in plain language.

**Side-character law**
Side characters often carry a single thematic function with a hard silhouette and a small verbal loop (Pagaya’s “sorry,” Gaimon’s chest silhouette). They are not mini-protagonists; they’re clean instruments that still feel human.

**Rival law**
A rival should mirror the protagonist’s dream vector but disagree on what “freedom” costs (methods, pride, cruelty, collateral). Rival presence is less about backstory density and more about *friction compatibility*: can they share a scene and ignite? This is consistent with Oda’s focus on characters “doing as they please,” creating emergent conflict.

**Villain law**
Villains should be difficult to fully explain; “sketchy” can be part of their charm/mystery. Use infrastructure (kingdoms, markets, prisons, labs) to make menace environmental. Doflamingo and Kaido are both framed as rulers and systems, not just fighters.

## One Piece naming rulebook and practical creation template

### One Piece naming rulebook

**Pirate names**
- Favor names that can carry both **myth** and **mockery**: they should survive being shouted with awe *and* being shortened into a joke. Smoker’s “Smoyan” effect shows how nicknames inject intimacy or disrespect.
- Add a **public handle** (epithet/通称) that is what the world prints first (“Straw Hat,” “Beasts,” “Soul King”).
- Keep phonetics sharp; Oda explicitly cares enough about naming to cite real pirate origins.

**Marine names**
- Marine identity often reads as: *rank structure + codename/epithet + one behavioral contradiction*. “White Hunter” is clearer and more One Piece than a generic “Captain ____.”
- Bake in the institution’s internal nicknaming culture to avoid “sterile military.”

**Government and Cipher Pol names**
- Use **coded organizational labels** (CP numbers, “Aigis,” etc.) but attach one absurdly memorable tag (animal companion, mask, petty ambition).
- Avoid over-sleek “ninja assassin” naming; bureaucracy in One Piece is often theatrical and petty.

**Island names**
- Island naming in One Piece often communicates a **theme machine**: a single concept the island expresses (season, food, technology level, cultural parody). The official database constantly ties characters to islands/kingdoms as identity context.
- Avoid generic fantasy geography (“Eldoria,” “Valethor”). One Piece islands frequently feel like an *idea you can draw*.

**Villain names**
- Villain names can be grand, but One Piece often pairs grandeur with a **toyetic system** (Mr.0/Mr.1; family naming schemes; disaster epithets).
- Do not over-explain the villain in the name. SBS guidance supports the utility of “sketchy mystery.”

**Crew names**
- Crew/org names often act like *brands* (delivery companies, newspapers, armies). Morgans’s corporation is literally his power identity.

**Attack names**
- One Piece uses wordplay heavily, including punning and idiosyncratic readings in Japanese (with adaptation/translation challenges). This reinforces that naming is not decoration; it’s part of the comedy/sound layer.

**Newspaper headlines**
- Headlines favor **big nouns + exclamation energy**, and the world has an actual “Big News” character whose entire identity is press power. Build your OC so a newspaper can distort them into myth.

**Epithets and bounty titles**
- Best epithets are what *outsiders notice first*: silhouette object (“Straw Hat”), social stigma (“Devil Child”), job (“White Hunter”), disaster category (“Wildfire,” “Drought”).
- Let misunderstanding be part of the system (Usopp/Sogeking; bounty misreporting). Epithets often become “sticky” when the world is wrong in an interesting way.

**Patterns to avoid**
- “The Shadow Emperor of Oblivion”-style titles (no diegetic source, no mockability, no social friction).
- Names that cannot be shortened, mispronounced, or turned into a gag nickname.
- Codenames that feel like generic spy fiction rather than One Piece’s playful bureaucratic toybox.

### Practical creation template for an authentic One Piece character

This template is operational: each step corresponds to one grammar channel and includes a validation test.

**Silhouette first**
- Choose **one dominant shape** (circle/box/spear/coil/tower) and **one inseparable prop** (mask, hat, weapon, coat drape, hair mass).
- Add **one body logic rule** (cyborg modularity like Franky; animal-body identity like Morgans; impossible proportions like many captains).
**Test:** Can a reader identify them in a black silhouette next to five other characters?

**Name hook**
- Pick a naming mode:
  - *Public-handle mode* (epithet-led like “Straw Hat”)
  - *System mode* (Mr./Miss numbers; CP labels)
  - *Theatrical profession mode* (“Big News,” “delivery,” “queen,” etc.)
- Add at least one of: phonetic punch, cultural salt, or pun elasticity.
**Test:** Can the name be mocked, shortened, and headlined?

**Compressed emotional truth**
- Write a single sentence: “This person is ______.” Not a backstory—an emotional thesis (Bellemere = “defiant chosen mother”; Laboon = “waiting as devotion”).
**Test:** Does this truth show up in how they behave when buying bread?

**Contradiction hinge**
- Define the visible dichotomy (appearance vs deeds; tone vs violence; loyalty vs cruelty).
**Test:** Can you demonstrate the contradiction in one panel of dialogue?

**Comedy engine**
- Build a loop: trigger → reaction → escalation.
  - Trigger examples from canon: insecurity button (Buggy’s nose), habitual phrase (Kureha, Pagaya), identity misunderstanding (Duval), catchphrase bravado (Franky).
**Test:** Can the loop fire in three different tones (comic, tense, tragic) without breaking character?

**Speech flavor**
- Choose: sentence length, register, one tic, and optionally a laugh style. Distinct laugh-writing is a recognized One Piece convention.
**Test:** Could you identify the speaker from one speech balloon with the name removed?

**Dream vector**
- Convert desire into a noun phrase target (panacea; true history; end of the sea; reunion).
**Test:** Could a child reader repeat the dream accurately after one scene?

**Wound placement**
- Specify how the wound changes casual behavior (Koala suppresses emotion; Robin distrusts; Chopper fears being a monster).
**Test:** What does the character do *wrong* in normal life because of the wound?

**Lovable / annoying / dangerous knobs**
- Assign one lovable cue (loyalty/mercy/sincerity), one annoying cue (petty trigger/tic), one danger cue (competence or ruthless capacity).
**Test:** In a single scene, can all three cues appear without feeling inconsistent?

**Iconic object lock**
- Choose one object whose removal would erase the character (hat, mask, pigeon, medical bag, instrument).
**Test:** Can you draw only the object + hand posture and still evoke them?

**Epithet and bounty phrasing**
- Write the epithet as something the world would say, not the character. Use the outsider’s first impression: object, stigma, job, disaster category, or misunderstanding.
**Test:** Can the epithet plausibly appear on a poster even if the character hates it?

**Final “fake-ness” audit**
- If the character is only “cool,” add an annoyance lever.
- If the character is only “weird,” add a clean emotional thesis.
- If the character is only “tragic,” add a repeatable comedic or social behavior loop.
