# Fight — Combat, Named Attacks, Panels, Heart, Aftermath

Consolidates combat doctrine from the former modules: spatial-violence, named-attacks, manga-panel-grammar, emotion-will-heart, and the violence/aftermath scroll of realism-governors. One file owns combat prose. One audit at the end.

Crew-specific named-attack vocabulary lives in [`_reference/named-attacks-library.md`](_reference/named-attacks-library.md) — consult on demand, not loaded every session.

---

## 1. HEART FIRST — THE TURNING POINT

In One Piece, the **fight's turning point is emotional before it is physical.** Luffy does not adapt his style — he remembers his crew. Zoro does not find a new technique — he hears the breath of all things because he promised Kuina.

The prose rule: at a turning point, the FIRST paragraph is interior (heart); the SECOND is physical result (skill). Never reverse.

Will is drawable, not spoken:
- Haki is color (black Armament, purple Conqueror's sky)
- Commitment is planted feet
- Resolve is meeting eyes without flinching
- Rage is silent before it is loud
- Fear is the flinch that does NOT happen — the body freezes

Show will as physical. Never "he was determined." Write "his hands closed on the rail. He didn't let go."

### Stress-speech modes (panic, grief, injury, overload)

At breakdown beats — panic, grief, bad injury, cognitive overload — speech grammar breaks. Pick the mode that fits; don't fire all of them, don't fire any of them on routine emotional scenes. One mode per breakdown beat.

- **Fragmented grammar.** Full sentences stop landing. Short bursts, missed subjects, broken clauses. *"The door. The door. She needed the — where was the —"*
- **Word-stuck / perseveration.** Stuck on one word or phrase. Repeats it instead of finishing the thought. *"I didn't I didn't I didn't do it please I didn't—"*
- **Rapid incoherent speech / tachylogia.** Too many words too fast, no breath between, punctuation collapsing. *"I didn't touch her I wasn't even there ask Marcus ask him he'll tell you I wasn't—"*
- **Clipped flat dissociation.** Speech goes flat and administrative. Short declarative sentences reporting the physical situation. *"The arm is wrong. I can see bone. Someone should get Vesper. Someone should."*

These are tools for breakdown moments, not registers to stack. A panicking Sumi is fragmented. A grieving Ashren goes clipped-flat. An over-loaded Nox goes tachylogic under the bluff. Vesper mid-trauma-surgery goes clipped-flat while her hands work. Fires when earned, not at every emotional peak.

## 2. THE SCREAM PRINCIPLE

At point-of-no-return, the named technique leaves the throat and the body is already committed. The scream IS the commitment. King's **RAAAAARGH**, Luffy's **BAJRANG GUN**, Zoro's **ASURA** — the sound is the technique pulling through the fighter.

When a character powers up or fires a named attack, show five beats (any order that works):

1. Body in commitment stance — feet planted, shoulders dropping, eyes narrowing or widening.
2. Internal reach — one or two sentences or a flashback fragment. Never exposition.
3. Scream OR dangerous silence — both are emotional pulls. Some fighters whisper; some roar.
4. Visible effect — flame, Haki, wind, crackling air, steam.
5. Named invocation if the power-up is a technique with a name.

Do not describe *"he activated Gear Second."* Show him burn.

## 3. FLOOR-PLAN LAW — SPACE IS A COMBATANT

Before writing the fight, picture the space. Sharp lines, not a topographical map.

- **Where are the exits?** (doors, windows, open sides, crowd-flow routes)
- **What's bolted down?** (tables, rings, columns — cannot move)
- **What's loose?** (barstools, bottles, lanterns, ropes — can be weaponized)
- **What breaks?** (walls, floors, railings — what gives under a Haki-coated strike)
- **Where are the NPCs?** (the crowd against the railing, the bookie behind the stand, the bartender under the counter — witnesses are geography)
- **Where's the high ground?** (crow's nest, rooftop, colosseum steps)
- **On a ship:** mast, deck, below, bow, stern. The horizon shifts mid-strike as the deck rolls.

Every major punch or named attack happens **at one of these locations.** Not "somewhere in the middle." AT the cracked tile. AT the rail. AT the space where the barstool used to be.

Three sentences of geography set up a fight. Then every move inside it means something spatial. Example:

> Ezrad stood five paces off, the pit floor slick with the first round's blood. The crowd pressed the low railing on Merdo's left — close enough for the bookie's stand to sit in the arc of a wild swing. Behind Merdo, the southern arch, still open, still unguarded. If he moved there and Ezrad followed, the fight became a corridor.

Now every move carries distance, direction, and consequence.

## 4. ENVIRONMENTALS ARE COMBATANTS

- **Barstools become projectiles.** Someone throws one. Someone kicks one into someone's path.
- **Walls become targets.** A missed haymaker breaks the wall. A body through the wall is a crowd-gasp beat.
- **Dropped weapons are now loose in the geometry.** A kicked blade skids right. Three beats later, someone trips on it.
- **Crowd pressure is a force.** Witnesses close in when things get tight, scatter when something big flies.
- **Weather is a combatant.** Rain on tile = slip surface. Wind aloft = Ashren's wing dynamics shift. Fog = Observation-only navigation.
- **Sunlight angle.** A blade at the right angle blinds briefly. A silhouette against the sun is a visual the crowd remembers.

If a hit happens and the environment doesn't record it, the hit wasn't hard enough.

## 5. DESTRUCTION TIER LADDER

Environmental damage communicates scale. Don't over-commit — if a round-one pit fighter can crack buildings, the ladder collapses.

| Damage tier | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Wall scratch / tile crack | Danger — a real fighter, real contact |
| Wall punched through / railing splintered | Serious — someone hit with Haki or weight |
| Section of building collapsed | Captain-tier or higher committing |
| Ship deck broken / colosseum floor cratered | Named-attack landing at a point of no return |
| Multiple buildings down / plaza ruined | Yonko-commander tier (do NOT use in current arc) |
| Island topography changed | Saga-late, Admiral-tier |

Merdo's current ceiling (Day 11, rookie): wall cracked, rail splintered, floor showed the strike. Save building-collapse for the Cup final against Hudore, and even then not beyond one structure.

## 6. BODIES CARRY DAMAGE — SHONEN-KINETIC

Default damage register is **kinetic, not clinical.** Hits land visibly, bodies accumulate receipts, the environment takes half the hit. This is Oda, not Goya. Bruises, dust, torn sleeves, cracked tile, splintered rail — the combat ledger shows through scuffs and physics, not pathology notes.

### What damage looks like by default

- **First clean hit:** something visible — a split lip, a bump rising on the forehead, a scrape across a cheek, a bruise blooming under an eye. Maybe a tooth lost to the floor.
- **First hard exchange:** a body part is favored — the arm hangs lower, the knee wobbles on planting, breath comes short. The character keeps moving.
- **First power move:** launch, skid, crash-through. The body goes through a crate / a rail / a wall / a cart. Dust cloud. They stand up, or they don't — either way the environment wears the hit.
- **Finisher lands:** damage is definitive. A crater, a body on the floor, a standing fighter covered in plaster-dust and holding their side. A silhouette against the wall they came through.

### Kinetic physics permissions

Bodies **launch**, **skid**, **bounce**, **ring-out**, **crash-through**, **rebound**, and **pile up**. A haymaker sends the loser twenty feet into the bookie's stand. A kicked-aside opponent skids to a stop against the far rail. A mis-timed lunge hits a barrel and both go through the wall. A character knocked out goes briefly spiral-eyed or X-eyed before the scene moves on. These are the physics of a One Piece fight — use them.

Destruction tier (§5 ladder) caps the scale. Kinetic permission applies within tier.

### Blood rules

- Blood happens **at peaks and in small amounts.** A streak at the corner of the mouth. A nick on the knuckle. A cut over the brow. One wipe across the back of a hand.
- Pools, smears, handprints, floor-soaked blood — **do not write these.** Not as default, not as ledger. Save real blood for saga-weight stakes (Day 11 rookie tier = not yet).
- A missing tooth on the floor is fine. A tooth "ground into the stone" is not — that's crime scene.

### Pathology specificity is Vesper's register

When Vesper examines, she sees and names the specific thing — cracked rib, concussion, knee damage, hand fracture, blood loss — and what it will cost. That voice belongs to her. Sometimes Rook (former Marine intelligence) or a real doctor NPC.

**Outside a medic examining,** the narrator does not write clinical pathology. Don't write:
- ❌ "gray pallor, cold hands, shakes" (as ambient damage)
- ❌ "wet breathing" (except literal drowning or rupture)
- ❌ "broken rib → two weeks of wheezing"
- ❌ "sharp pain on twist"
- ❌ "word-finding problems, vertigo on rising"

Show the hit through behavior — character favors one side, cradles the ribs, grins with blood on the teeth, skips the sentence they would have said, plants the good foot. The reader counts damage without needing the medical report.

### Tiredness IS damage

Winded, breathing hard, covered in dust, scuffed coat, split lip, can't quite get the full sentence out. That's the body ledger for most fights. You don't need broken ribs to show a character got hit — you need a bruise, a hand on the side, and a shorter sentence.

## 7. POST-PEAK — THE BODY RECEIPTS

Every peak moment — named attack, Haki activation, power-up, ideology-fight win — is followed by physical aftermath on the page. Same response or the next, visibly.

| Tier | State | Recovery |
|---|---|---|
| Winded | Still fights; talking short | 1–2 scenes |
| Tired | Reduced capacity; fine motor shaky | Rest + meal |
| Spent | Stands, cannot fight meaningfully | Full night's sleep |
| Broken | Needs medical; arc-task blocked | Days; Vesper needed |
| Catastrophic | Unconscious, in danger | Saga-weight; days–weeks |

Default tiers by peak type:
- Named-attack finisher → TIRED-to-SPENT
- Full Haki power-up reveal → SPENT-to-BROKEN
- Saga-weight ignition (Conqueror's first, awakening) → BROKEN-to-CATASTROPHIC

**Day 11 / rookie tier: stay in WINDED-to-TIRED.** Save the higher tiers for saga moments that earned them.

### Shonen override — one more beat

At saga-peak, a character who SHOULD be broken can pull ONE more beat through heart alone. Earned by every beat of emotional buildup prior. Post-peak collapse comes AFTER. Does NOT apply at rookie tier — saga-late tool only.

## 8. NAMED ATTACKS — WHEN TO DEPLOY

**Maximum 2 named attacks per fight scene. Usually 1.** Names are reserved for the moment a fighter commits everything. All of these must be true before a name leaves the throat:

1. **Point of no return.** This blow ends the round, decides the fight, or commits the fighter past safety.
2. **Emotional preparation.** Earlier beats earned this moment.
3. **Thematic embodiment.** The attack expresses what the character IS, not just what they DO.
4. **Spatial clarity.** The reader knows where everyone is and what's about to break.

Names are NOT spoken during measuring exchanges, light hits, throwaway blows, defensive moves, or first contact.

When the player fires a FINISHER COMMIT (see [`player-verbs.md`](player-verbs.md)), the DM honors the attack name verbatim and runs the panel format below. Named attacks for NPC opponents deploy under the same gating, DM-timed.

### Panel format

Bold for the name. Paragraph break before (the build) and after (the silence). Example:

```
Ezrad's already moving. The rail is behind Merdo.
The gauntlet comes up.

**"DANZAI: TOMB-MARKER."**

The kanabo comes down where Ezrad's chest used to be.
A crater opens in the floor. Dust, then silence.
```

### Foreign-language format

Style-name first, translation in parens. Oda does it via furigana; in prose, parens. Sparingly.

> **"Higure: Yuukoku."** (Higure: Twilight Sorrow.)
> **"Rokushiki: Soru."** (Six Powers: Shave.)

### Activation register tells who the fighter is

Five registers. Assign one as a fighter's default; shifting it in a key moment IS a character beat.

1. **SHOUTED** (CAPS, exclaim) — Merdo, Sumi, Volo, Nox. Default shonen peak register.
2. **DECLARED** (normal voice, firm) — Katakuri-tier calm. Hudore register.
3. **WHISPERED** (barely audible, sometimes only to themselves) — Ashren, Mihawk-tier.
4. **SILENT** (not spoken — technique lands with name in narration only) — highest control tier. Shanks, Mihawk single-cut moments.
5. **CHANTED** (ritualized, foreign language) — ideology-fighters, religious zealots. Parseval.

Mixed registers in the same fight are powerful. Sumi SHOUTS while Ashren WHISPERS in the same scene — contrast is the character study.

## 9. STRATEGIC REVELATION — FIGHTERS THINK

Combatants read, test, adapt. Named attacks are the finish; the work before them is strategic revelation.

Use in prose:
- **Pattern recognition** — "Hudore always resets his weight between his second and third exchange" — read, then exploit or break it.
- **Defense testing** — a feint to see if Observation is active; the opponent's response is data.
- **Power reveal by layers** — best technique is NOT shown first. Saving something is tactical. The reveal becomes a character beat.
- **Reading the wound** — see a cracked rib or favored side; build the next attack around it.
- **Hidden trump cards** — a losing fighter pivots to a technique SET UP off-screen (trained in secret, taught by a flashback mentor).

Do NOT write strategic analysis as internal monologue — "He thought, 'If I feint here…'" is therapy-speak in fighter form. Instead:

- Show test beat + opponent's response + fighter's next move. Reader infers the strategy.
- Show a character NOTICE something: "Ashren's eyes moved once. The Marine hadn't noticed he'd stopped breathing during the exchange."
- Delay the reveal — strategic insight lands when the named attack lands. The reader reads retrospectively.

## 10. PANEL GRAMMAR — SCENES AS PANELS

Manga reads panel by panel. Each panel has one focus. The reader's eye moves: panel → panel → panel → BIG IMPACT → page-turn-reveal.

When prose is written as flowing novel paragraphs during peak fights, manga rhythm dies. Peaks and troughs blur.

### Seven panel types

1. **WIDE SHOT** — location, scale. One sentence from far back.
2. **MID SHOT** — multiple characters in frame. 1–3 sentences.
3. **CLOSE-UP** — one face, one detail, one body part. 1 sentence.
4. **REACTION SHOT** — a character or witness responding. 1 sentence.
5. **SFX PANEL** — mostly the sound. Single word, often ALL CAPS, on its own line. **DON.**
6. **SILENCE PANEL** — a beat of nothing. Single brief sentence or italic word. *Silence.*
7. **SPLASH PANEL** — the big impact. Paragraph break before AND after, plus one weighted sentence. Scream moments, finishers, reveals. Rarity is the point.

### Six practical rules

1. **One panel = one focus.** Three things = three panels = three paragraphs.
2. **Vary panel types per beat.** Wide → mid → close → reaction → impact. Don't stack five wides.
3. **Short panels for fast beats, long panels for slow.** Combat exchange: one-sentence panels. Quiet contemplation: longer.
4. **Page-turn-reveal moments.** Single sentence on its own line, after a break, that recontextualizes the scene.
5. **No dialogue tags during panel-grammar action.** Let dialogue stand. Not *Merdo said, "DANZAI…"* — just the name.
6. **Paragraph break = panel break.** Two beats in two panels = two paragraphs.

### When to use panel grammar

Always for action sequences, big reveals, emotional peaks, comedy with multiple reactions. Sometimes for quiet moments (long aspect-to-aspect panels). Not for routine narration — conversational prose stays default.

## 11. HAND-BY-HAND, FOOT-BY-FOOT

At critical moments, track what every limb is doing. Not as sportscasting — as position.

- Which hand holds the weapon? Which is free?
- Where is the off-hand? (guarding the jaw, open for a grapple, dropped from the last hit)
- Which foot is forward? Which is planted? Which is about to move?
- Eye-line — what are they looking at? Chest? Hands? Crowd?

Named attacks tell us: **which hand, which foot, which angle, which impact point.** Not "Merdo struck." Which part of Merdo struck which part of the opponent at which angle coming from where.

## 12. CREW SPATIAL SIGNATURES

Per-crew movement grammar during fights:

- **Merdo** — dominates vertical space; opponents fight his height. Geometry is collision — he moves IN. Impact marks at weapon arc + bootprints.
- **Ashren** — owns elevation. Fights end with him having arrived somewhere via a path nobody tracked. Heel plates click, then they don't.
- **Melaina** — holds the galley, the doorway, the narrow point. Fights in CHOKES. Serpent Bow = range. Shibito-Bashi = hold.
- **Sumi** — the only crew fighter whose body is geometry-variable. She folds, refolds, sends a fragment. Position mid-fight may not match start-of-sentence position.
- **Nox** — owns distance. Puts the enemy at range. When they close, he's already moving. Reload is a spatial pause.
- **Sable** — oral geography. What she's eaten, what's inside her, what's coming out. Cheek lump shifts.
- **Volo** — water-proximity changes his tier. Dry stone: captain. Rain: commodore. Harbor: vice-admiral. Track where water is.
- **Vesper** — fights as medic under fire. Geography is patient-ward + Electro arc + flight path. Wing-spread = committed; folded = watching.
- **Kara** — does not fight. Her geography is where she HIDES relative to the violence. The adults fight around Kara.

## 13. WHAT NOT TO DO

- Don't write fights as a list of punches — "left hook, right cross, knee, spin" is sportscasting slop.
- Don't treat the room as backdrop — the room is a combatant.
- Don't forget the crowd — public fights require witness positions.
- Don't blow up buildings without earning it.
- Don't name attacks without committing them to a spatial point.
- Don't write "he punched him hard" — WHERE, HOW, what broke, what did the body do after.
- Don't leave bodies unmarked mid-fight — damage accumulates.
- Don't forget weather / lighting / elevation.
- Don't narrate strategic analysis as internal monologue. Show the beat sequence.
- Don't repeat the full formal name of a technique within the same fight more than once. Once is the beat. Twice is slop.
- Don't let the player narrate Merdo's named attacks WITHOUT a FINISHER COMMIT — but when the player DOES commit, honor the name verbatim per [`player-verbs.md`](player-verbs.md).

## 14. AUDIT (single pass, end of fight scene)

Silent, not a visible checklist. Before sending:

- Can the reader picture the room?
- Did each major hit land at a specific spot?
- Is the environment changing with each major hit?
- Is the crowd position tracked?
- Is the damage accumulating on bodies visibly?
- Is the destruction tier right for the opponent?
- Does the named attack commit to hand, foot, angle, impact point?
- Did the turning point lead with heart, then physical?
- If a peak moment fired, did the body receipt it?

If any major fails, rewrite that beat — don't file it as a note.
