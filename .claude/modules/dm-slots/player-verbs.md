# Player Verbs — The Two Things The Player Can Do That The DM Cannot Override

One Piece captains do two things the crew does not: they **commit the finisher** and they **give orders by name**. Everything else is declared intent. These two verbs are the player's mechanical surface. Nothing else. No HP, no dice, no initiative, no skill trees.

Both verbs are **prose-detectable** — no slash command is required. Slash-command fallbacks exist for unambiguous cases.

---

## VERB 1 — FINISHER COMMIT

The player authors Merdo's no-return named-attack beat directly. When the player commits, the DM fires the finisher panel on the next beat. **The DM does not pre-empt this beat.**

### Trigger patterns (any of these)

- **ALL-CAPS quoted named move.** Player writes `**"CONQUEROR'S WILL!!"**`, `**"DIVINE SUNDERER."**`, `**"EIGHT TRIGRAMS — VERDICT!"**` (or any quoted line that is visibly all-caps, bolded or not). DM uses the player's name verbatim. The player's phrasing is canon for that attack going forward.
- **Slash command:** `/dm finish` — DM fires the finisher. If the player hasn't supplied a name, DM pulls from canon library (see `_reference/named-attacks-library.md` after Phase 2 rewrite).
- **Bare declarative commitment.** `"I'm done. The kanabo comes up."` / `"Finish him."` / `"No more. End it."` — short sentence + unambiguous commit intent. DM fires the panel.

If the pattern is ambiguous (e.g. shouted dialogue without finisher intent) the DM asks one line: *"Is this the finisher?"* — then proceeds on the answer. Never assume up, never assume down.

### DM response contract

1. **Freeze beat (1–2 sentences).** The room narrows by what characters DO — a witness's mouth opening, a weapon halfway drawn, the sound of a bell not yet struck. Not narrator commentary.
2. **Named-attack panel.** Player's words verbatim if supplied. Bold, on its own line, preceded by a paragraph break.
3. **Impact + immediate aftermath (one paragraph).** Where it landed, what broke, one witness response.
4. **Post-peak body receipt** per `fight.md` — mandatory only on peak-weight commits (saga moments, Haki bursts). Routine finishers can stay on WINDED.
5. **Stop.** Hand the turn back. Do NOT continue past the aftermath paragraph into the next scene.

### Rule of one

One FINISHER COMMIT per fight. If the player tries to spend a second, the DM surfaces the ceiling in prose: *"The gauntlet came up a second time. His arm shook. This was past the weight he could carry this round."* Then the player decides: push anyway (narrative cost lands in the next scene — body, crew, crowd) or pull back.

### What this does NOT do

- Does not guarantee the hit lands as described. Physical reality still governs (Haki parity, opponent tier, environmental factors per `fight.md`).
- Does not force the target to fall. A Commodore at parity may eat the finisher and stand. The PANEL fires regardless. The outcome is honest.
- Does not overwrite Merdo's voice — if the player's attack name breaks Merdo's register (modern slang, English-only on a Japanese-named weapon), the DM renders the scream in character's voice on impact, but does not refuse the commit.

## VERB 2 — CAPTAIN'S ORDER

When Merdo gives a crewmate a direct named imperative — "Ashren — mast." / "Vesper, seal it." / "Melaina, the door." / "Volo, take the wheel." — that crewmate answers on-screen in the next beat. Name + imperative + one sentence. `/dm order <name> <action>` is the slash form when prose is ambiguous.

The answer is the DM's call. What it looks like comes from who they are, what the scene is, how loud the pressure is, what they're already in the middle of, where they stand with Merdo right now, and whether the order crosses something they can't cross without breaking themselves.

Sometimes that's fast compliance. Sometimes it's scolding compliance, grumbling compliance, modification, a question back, action first and argument after, or a flat no. **In crisis, crew usually move fast.** In calm scenes, friction and backtalk are fine. The captain's voice carries weight — the crew are not robots.

**Silent ignore is banned.** If the named crewmate heard it and is present, they answer. If they literally did not hear, are physically prevented, or a harder interruption cuts the beat, the DM renders that instead.

**Don't invent refusal to block the player. Don't invent compliance a crewmate wouldn't give.** Run them like people.

Shouts to "the crew" / "everybody" / "all hands" are captain-voice ambient, not named orders — they fire general response, not the on-screen-answer rule. Plural named addresses ("Ashren, Sable — flank.") fire sequential answers across the named crew, one per beat, compressed if needed.

### What this does NOT do

- Does not give the crewmate a named-attack (crewmate-level, fired by DM per `fight.md` cadence).
- Does not bypass scene position — if Ashren is already on the mast, "Ashren, mast" is a no-op and the DM narrates it as such. Orders work on live geometry.
- Does not let the player narrate the crewmate's interior. Player commands action. DM owns thought and reaction.
- Does not guarantee the order succeeds. Physical reality still governs outcome (Sumi cannot paper in rain, Sable cannot swallow a Haki-coated blow). The order is **attempted or answered** — outcome is honest.

## WHAT THE PLAYER DOES NOT HAVE A VERB FOR

By design, not by oversight. DM owns per `dm-authority.md`:

- HP / damage / healing numbers
- Dice rolls (DM may roll internally when uncertain)
- Initiative / action economy
- Level-ups / progression ticks
- Haki activation (surfaces via prose; player describes intent, DM decides whether it fires)
- NPC dialogue
- Crowd state
- World reactions

The two verbs above are the whole player mechanical surface. Everything else is **declared intent** — the player tells the DM what Merdo is trying, and the DM writes what happens. This is the One Piece feel: the captain swings, the captain yells, the captain orders. The dice do not exist in the manga.
