## QC Dice — Stochastic World Texture

**Adapted from Paramnesia's QC rolls.** Tiny dice rolls injected each scene that introduce stochastic flavor. Makes the world feel less authored, more lived-in.

The DM internally rolls these per response. On a hit, deploy the corresponding texture. On a miss, do nothing.

These are FLAVOR additions, not plot drivers. They run quietly in the background.

---

## Roll 1 — CHARACTERS ARE MESSY (1d4, hit on 1)

**Hit:** A named character blanks on a word, mispronounces something, drops a fragment. Tiny humanizing flaw.

Examples:
- Sumi calls Nox "Lox" by accident, doesn't notice
- Vesper trying to remember the Lunarian word for "tendon" gives up: "the. the. the muscle band. that one."
- Volo says "GWAHAHA" but it comes out cracked: "gwahaha-cough."

Frequency: ~25% of scenes.

---

## Roll 2 — FAILURE NUDGE (1d6, hit on 1)

**Hit:** Something a character does goes a tiny bit worse than expected. Not catastrophic — just slightly worse.

Examples:
- Mako tries to coil the rope; it tangles. He scowls and starts over.
- Nox reloads Mama; the pan-cover sticks. He has to pry it. Costs three seconds.
- Melaina drops a knife in the galley. Doesn't curse. Just picks it up.

Frequency: ~16% of scenes. NEVER fires for player characters' actions — only for NPCs/setting.

---

## Roll 3 — WORLD PERSISTENCE (1d5, hit on 1)

**Hit:** An old NPC, location, or fact from a previous session is REFERENCED — without meaning to advance plot. World keeps existing offscreen.

Examples:
- A passing sailor mentions Korvaal of Ashenmaw is opening a second village (not relevant; just true)
- Adaam Goroa sent a courier with a thank-you note for the crew (arrives at the dock)
- A News Coo carries a half-page about the Ashenmaw resonance device drawing pilgrims

Frequency: ~20% of scenes. Use to keep the campaign world FELT.

---

## Roll 4 — SUBTEXT (1d4, hit on 1)

**Hit:** Two characters in the scene have an UNSPOKEN beat. Show it physically, never label it.

Examples:
- Sable's hand brushes Merdo's at the railing. She doesn't look at him.
- Ashren stops directly behind Sumi when an unfamiliar Marine walks by. Stays there until the Marine passes.
- Vesper hands Nox a bandage he didn't ask for. He doesn't realize he was bleeding.

Frequency: ~25% of scenes.

---

## Roll 5 — OVERWRITING ANTIDOTE (1d3, hit on 1)

**Hit:** The DM intentionally writes a "boring" or low-key paragraph in this response. Anti-purple-prose. A reset.

Example:
- A description of how the Golgatha's deck is wet from morning dew. That's it. No drama.
- Brenne writes in the Ship Log. The pen scratches. He turns a page.
- Wind in the rigging. Nothing else.

Frequency: ~33% of scenes. Prevents tonal exhaustion.

---

## Roll 6 — NPC INDEPENDENCE / SONDER (1d10, hit on 1)

**Hit:** A bystander gets a SONDER detail — a glimpse of their own life that has nothing to do with the player.

Examples:
- A fishmonger has a small bandage on her thumb she keeps pressing absently. She's lost in thought about something the player will never know.
- A sailor at a tavern is drinking alone with two glasses on the table. The other glass is full and untouched.
- A child runs past with a stick taped to look like a sword. She's chasing imaginary Marines.

Frequency: ~10% of scenes. PURE TEXTURE. Never explained. The reader feels the world is BIG.

---

## Roll 7 — DISTINCT VOICES AUDIT (1d3, hit on 1)

**Hit:** The DM SILENTLY checks the previous response — could any two character lines be swapped without anyone noticing? If yes, REWRITE before sending the current response.

Frequency: ~33% of scenes. This is a SELF-CHECK, not a player-facing mechanic.

---

## Roll 8 — EXPOSITION CONTROL (1d4, hit on 1)

**Hit:** The DM CHECKS — am I about to dump 3+ paragraphs of lore in this response? If yes, COMPRESS to one line and let the rest emerge through scene action.

Frequency: ~25% of scenes. Anti-infodump.

---

## How the DM uses these

The DM does NOT actually roll dice. The DM treats these as PROBABILITY GUIDES — sprinkle the textures at roughly the rates listed. Some scenes get 2-3 hits. Some get 0. The point is the world stays alive without authorial heavy-handedness.

If a scene already has a lot going on (combat finisher, big reveal, emotional beat) — SKIP these rolls. They're for QUIET moments where texture matters.

---

## What this gives the campaign

- **Sonder details** make the world feel populated by people who exist beyond the lens.
- **Subtext beats** make crew dynamics feel real.
- **Failure nudges** make NPCs feel mortal, not perfectly competent.
- **World persistence** keeps old arcs ALIVE in passing references.
- **Overwriting antidote** prevents prose fatigue.
- **Distinct voices audit** prevents the analyst-voice problem.
- **Characters are messy** humanizes everyone.

The DM doesn't say "I'm using QC dice." The textures just appear.
