## One-Shot Mini-Arcs — The Gaimon Pattern

Oda inserts **one-shot characters with complete mini-arcs inside larger arcs.** These characters appear, get their whole story told in 1-3 chapters, then exit. They're not crew, they're not recurring villains — they're complete-on-their-own stories.

Examples:
- **Gaimon** on Jaya — trapped in a treasure chest, guarding a treasure that doesn't exist, waiting for pirates who never come. One arc. Gone.
- **Norland the Liar** — dead 400 years. His story is TOLD inside the Jaya/Skypiea arc. Complete mini-arc via flashback.
- **Dr. Hiriluk** — died before the Straw Hats arrive. Whole story told inside Drum Arc. One of Oda's most devastating one-shot arcs.
- **Bellamy** — appears in Jaya as a dismissive antagonist, returns once in Dressrosa for his complete arc. Two appearances, one complete story.
- **Senor Pink** — minor Dressrosa antagonist, gets a SINGLE flashback chapter that breaks hearts. One arc inside an arc.
- **Gan Fall (God Enel's predecessor)** — Skypiea one-shot, complete arc.
- **Tonjit** (Davy Back Fight island) — horse-stilts-family-reunion. Complete one-shot.

This module gives the DM a template for dropping these in without overplanning.

---

## Why one-shot arcs MATTER

1. **They make the world feel BIGGER.** Oda's world isn't just Merdo's crew + their antagonists. Every island has people living their own lives with their own arcs.
2. **They teach the themes.** Gaimon teaches "dreams don't need treasure." Hiriluk teaches "a man dies when he is forgotten, not when he is shot." The one-shots carry the arc's thematic work.
3. **They deliver emotional punches** cheaply — because the reader invests in a one-shot fast and gets the payoff fast.
4. **They give the Merdo crew something OTHER than themselves to care about**, which is what TURNS a pirate crew into heroes.

---

## The one-shot arc template

A one-shot arc fits INTO 1-3 sessions / 1-5 scenes. Its structure:

### Beat 1 — SETUP (1 scene)
Introduce a weird person / setup in the current location. Something OFF:
- A kid who bows too low.
- An old sailor who keeps looking toward the same dock at the same time each day.
- A shopkeeper who refuses to sell the last item in their shop.
- A Marine who's obviously not happy being a Marine.
- A child with an absurd weapon-too-big-for-them.

The crew notices. The DM establishes the WEIRDNESS without explaining it.

### Beat 2 — BACKSTORY (1 scene, flashback or exposition)
The strangeness is explained — usually through flashback, usually through a second character the one-shot character trusts.

The backstory is small but complete. A short tragedy + a small hope.

### Beat 3 — CRISIS (1-2 scenes)
The one-shot character's situation comes to a head. Something decisive happens TODAY, in the crew's presence. They can help — or walk away.

### Beat 4 — RESOLUTION (1 scene)
The one-shot character's arc CLOSES. They get their small triumph or their small tragedy. The crew's help (or non-help) is the catalyst.

### Beat 5 — EXIT (1 paragraph or cover-page cut)
The one-shot character exits the crew's life. They might appear again much later — Bellamy-style, via one callback — but usually they don't.

---

## When to deploy a one-shot arc

- **Inside a main-arc.** Don't do a one-shot INSTEAD of a main arc. Do it AS A B-PLOT. Tessara's colosseum is the A-plot; a one-shot on the docks would be the B-plot.
- **Between major arcs.** Travel days between islands can be one-shot mini-arcs. (A Twenty member has a brother in the next port. A passing ship requests help with a small problem. A kid stowed away.)
- **When the crew is quiet.** If the player is in downtime mode, seed a one-shot character nearby and let them bite.

## One-shot archetypes (reach for these)

### The Waiting One
Someone who has been waiting for someone / something for years. Often alone. Sometimes dead.
- Laboon waiting 50 years for Brook.
- Tonjit waiting for his herd.
- A port widow who keeps a candle lit for a fisherman who's not coming back.

Arc shape: the crew RESOLVES the waiting — brings back the missing, confirms the death, OR joins the waiting briefly. The waiting one gets closure or peace.

### The Dying One
Someone living their last arc. Often still defiant. Often making a small final request.
- Dr. Hiriluk.
- Tom the shipwright (Water 7 flashback).
- An old Marine with a week left who needs one last thing delivered.

Arc shape: the crew becomes the instrument of their final wish. The Dying One dies with their small thing done.

### The Trapped One
Someone stuck in an absurd situation they've accepted.
- Gaimon in the chest.
- Mohji with his lion.
- A merchant whose "debt" keeps him in one port forever.

Arc shape: the crew either frees them OR discovers that the Trapped One doesn't WANT to be freed. Either resolution lands.

### The Legacy One
Someone carrying a legacy that doesn't belong to them.
- Montblanc Cricket descended from Norland the Liar.
- A monk guarding a temple whose deity nobody remembers.
- A child with their dead parent's sword, too big for them.

Arc shape: the crew helps them PASS IT ON or CLAIM IT. The legacy is resolved.

### The Regret One
Someone who made a mistake they can't undo.
- Ginny (pre-Kuma appearance, saga-late).
- A ship's captain who left his crew to die once and is looking for the chance to not leave one again.

Arc shape: the crew gives them a SECOND chance at the choice they got wrong. They either succeed (redemption) or fail (tragedy).

### The Lost One
Someone looking for something that can't be found the normal way.
- Brook looking for Laboon.
- Vivi looking for the traitors in Alabasta.
- A woman whose son is on a Marine ship she can't reach.

Arc shape: the crew ACCIDENTALLY helps them find it — by being in the right place, doing the right wrong thing.

## Session-level rhythm

Aim for: **one one-shot mini-arc per 2-3 major islands.** Not every island. Not every session. But often enough that the world feels populated beyond the crew's antagonists.

Tessara arc could EITHER:
- Have a one-shot (someone on the docks, someone in the colosseum crowd, a prisoner in The Cage who isn't central to the rescue arc)
- OR stay tight to the Cup + crew-decision threads and run a one-shot in the NEXT arc

DM's call based on pacing. If the session is feeling thin, drop a one-shot.

---

## What one-shots MUST NOT do

- Steal the spotlight from the main arc
- Become recurring without explicit user engagement (one-shots exit)
- Solve Merdo's problems FOR him
- Deliver saga-level reveals (one-shots are small)
- Be complicated — their whole arc fits in 1-3 sessions

---

## What one-shots GIVE

- The crew a reason to CARE about this island beyond the antagonist
- Thematic work cheaply
- World-feels-lived-in texture
- Callback potential (small — Gaimon still being there when Straw Hats return)
- Emotional beats without long-arc commitment

This is one of Oda's most efficient craft moves. Use it.
