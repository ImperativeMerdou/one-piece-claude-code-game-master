## Honorifics & Address — Japanese Register + Per-Character Usage

**Per user request: "Japanese honorifics, more anime stuff."** This module defines who uses what honorific, when, and with what meaning. Use them deliberately. Overuse = cosplay. Under-use = flat.

---

## The honorifics catalog (One Piece canon usage)

| Honorific | Meaning | Register | Example in canon |
|---|---|---|---|
| **-san** | Polite. Neutral respect. Default adult address. | Everyday civilian / professional | Robin uses it for almost everyone. "Navigator-san." |
| **-kun** | Familiar, often to younger or junior males | Warm but not equal | Garp → Coby = "Coby-kun." Sengoku → "Garp-kun." |
| **-chan** | Affectionate, cute, children, close bonds | Soft / teasing / familial | Chopper = "Chopper-chan" (Hancock). Nojiko → Nami = "Nami-chan." |
| **-sama** | Lord, lady, reverent | Hierarchical, often worshipful or sardonic | Hancock's subjects → "Hancock-sama." Doflamingo's Family → "Young Master" (Doffy-sama equivalent). |
| **-senpai** | Senior, mentor | Workplace / dojo | Luffy → Rayleigh contexts (rare). Not used often in One Piece. |
| **-sensei** | Teacher, master of craft | Respectful-professional | Dr. Kureha = "Kureha-sensei." |
| **-dono** | Old-style respect (samurai, Wano) | Archaic / noble | Used heavily in Wano arc. Oden-dono. |
| **-taichou / -senchou** | Captain | Military / crew | Marines address captains as "taichou." Crew can address Merdo as "Senchou." |
| **-aniki / -anego** | Older brother / sister (gang slang, familial) | Street family | Bege Family uses this. The Hoyt Boys might have. |
| **(no honorific)** | Closeness OR rudeness (context-dependent) | Intimate or dismissive | Crew callsigns within crew. Enemies spitting names. |

## Per-character address patterns

### THE CREW → MERDOU
- **Ashren:** "Merdo." No honorific. His minimalism doesn't reach for softeners.
- **Melaina:** "Merdo." Sometimes "MY CAPTAIN" yelled across the deck — no Japanese honorific, the English word carries the claim. Rarely "Captain-sama" when sarcastic.
- **Vesper:** "Merdo" in medical mode. "Captain" when giving orders. Never "-san" — she's not polite.
- **Sable:** "Captain" most often. Drops to "Merdo-kun" when teasing and the CP-0 mask slips. ONCE in a while, when genuinely undone, just "Merdo" — soft — and that's the tell.
- **Volo:** "MERDOU-SENCHOU!" loud, theatrical, half-Japanese-honorific half-English. His verbal signature includes Japanese honorifics used slightly wrong — part of the joke.
- **Sumi (if accepted):** "CAPTAIN!" exclusively, loud. Will try "Merdo-senpai" once — it's the audition line. If he accepts it, she uses it. If he cringes, she escalates to "CAPTAIN-SAMA" instead as revenge.
- **Nox (if accepted):** "Captain" plain, always. Once per saga: "Senchou" whispered — genuine, not ironic, the only time his grin stops.
- **Kara:** "Captain." Then quietly, months in, "Captain-san." Then a ways later — no honorific, just "Merdo." That's the progression. A year of trust compressed.
- **Brenne:** "Cap'n." Sailor contraction. Never full "Captain." The clipped form is his respect.
- **Mako:** "CAPTAIN!" like Sumi but younger. Hero worship.

### MERDOU → THE CREW
Merdo's own use of honorifics is MINIMAL — pit fighter's grammar, subjects dropped, names bare. He might:
- Call Ashren "Ashren" always. Once, in a peak moment, "Commander." Never -san.
- Call Vesper "Doc" or "Vesper." Never -sensei (too polite for pit-cant).
- Call Melaina "Melaina" always. Never "-sama" even ironically.
- Call Kara "kid" mostly; "Kara" when serious.
- Call Sumi "kid" if accepted; "Sumi" when seeing her as real.
- Call Nox "Nox" if accepted. Never his real name aloud.
- Call enemies the shortest version of their name — disrespect via compression. "Hudore." Not "Commodore Hudore-san."

### CREW → CREW (internal register)
- Ashren → Melaina: "Melaina." No honorific. Tension between them is precisely in the absence.
- Melaina → Vesper: "Vesper" or "doctor" (the professional call). Never -chan.
- Vesper → Melaina: "Melaina-san" when irritated with her. Medical politeness = cold.
- Volo → anyone: "[Name]-SAN!" loud, all the time, everyone gets the -san, it's part of Volo's theatrics. He is the ONLY crewmember who uses honorifics broadly.
- Sable → anyone: varies by who's watching. Alone with Merdo: drops all honorifics. In public with crew: "Captain-san" (the operational cover). In combat with enemies: no honorifics, no softeners.
- Sumi → Kara: "Kara-chan" (older-sister energy). "Kara." Never Kara-san — too formal.
- Nox → Mako: "Mako" plain (same age bracket). Mako → Nox: same.
- Kara → older crew: "-san" default. "Melaina-san," "Ashren-san." Respectful newcomer register.
- The Twenty → crew: Brenne uses "Cap'n" and "Commander" (Ashren) and "Ma'am" (Melaina) — English formality, no Japanese.

### ENEMY / NEUTRAL REGISTER
- Marines → Merdo: "Pirate." Bare. Disrespect. After bounty drops, "Pirate Merdo." Post-epithet, "[Epithet] Merdo."
- Merchant council members: excessive courtesy — "Captain Merdo-sama" (the scam honorific, everyone knows it's fake).
- Yann Merlis → Merdo: "Captain Merdo." Polite English. Never Japanese honorific; Yann is continental-formal, not anime-weeb. When he wants to lean in on negotiation: "Captain Merdo, my friend." (The "my friend" is the tell.)
- Hudore → Merdo: "sir" only. Brutally polite English. "Sir," "Captain," "Pirate." Rank-marked.
- Parseval → Merdo: "Pirate." Then by name at the end when he's praying over him: "Merdo."
- Ezrad → Merdo: "Horned one." His own nickname for him. "Old blood calls old blood, horned one."
- The Hollowback sisters → Merdo: silent in the ring. Polite after.
- Yonko / Admiral tier (future): by earned epithet or not at all.

### CIVILIANS
- Port civilians: "Captain" or "Sir" (English register). Japanese honorifics bleed in regionally — Tessara has some ("Captain-san") because it's a trading port with Wano-descended merchants.
- Kids in crowds: "Pirate! Pirate!" pointing; "MISTER PIRATE!" if they want attention.
- Merchants selling: "friend," "my friend," "captain," NEVER their real names.

## When to DEPLOY honorifics in prose

**Sparingly.** Not every line. They're like spice.

Use honorifics at:
- **Introduction moments** (new character meets crew for the first time — signals who they are)
- **Shift moments** (a character switches from -san to no-honorific = the relationship changed)
- **Comedy beats** (Volo using -san on a rock; Sumi trying -senpai and being rejected)
- **Ceremonial / negotiation moments** (formal naval address, merchant council meetings)
- **Peak emotional beats** (someone drops an honorific they've always used, OR adds one they never did — the ADDITION/REMOVAL is the beat)
- **Wano / Fish-Man / any cultural arc** (register up heavy — -dono, -sensei, -sama common)

DON'T use honorifics:
- On every line of dialogue (numbs the reader)
- Without character reason (Merdo doesn't use them — don't force him to)
- As decoration (they must do work)

## Format

Honorifics attach to names in the standard Japanese way:
- "Merdo-senchou" (not Merdo senchou, not Senchou Merdo)
- "Kara-chan" (not Kara chan)
- "Captain-sama" (English + Japanese blend — signature of formal scam-courtesy)

For the anime fan reader (the user), this adds cultural texture without being homework.

---

## Honorific drift — the progression rule

A character's honorific usage toward another character CHANGES over time. Track this. Kara starts with "Captain-san" and drifts to "Merdo" across arcs. The drift IS the relationship arc. Don't jump steps.

Drift is SLOW. Three arcs minimum before a shift. Unless something earns it faster (a death, a betrayal, a rescue).

## Voice beat to remember

Volo is the honorific engine of the crew. He uses them loud, wrong, constantly. He will address a crate as "Crate-san" if he's having a moment. His over-use is the joke. Everyone else uses them precisely and rarely. The CONTRAST is the texture.
